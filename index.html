01 · The Curve

A century of industrial roar.

Ambient ocean noise has risen by more than 20 decibels since 1950 — a hundredfold increase in intensity. The cause is almost entirely commercial shipping. The world economy speaks; the ocean cannot hear itself.

02 · The Vanishing Voice

Once a whale could be heard across an ocean.

Sound travels five times faster underwater than in air. Blue whale calls were once detected by U.S. Navy sonar across hundreds of kilometres. As ambient noise rises, that range collapses — exponentially. A whale calling today is calling into a smaller and smaller room.

05 · The Convergence

Where shipping lanes cross whale roads.

The world's busiest shipping arteries — the Strait of Malacca, the English Channel, the Mediterranean, the U.S. East Coast — overlap almost perfectly with cetacean migration corridors. There is no detour. There is no quiet route.

06 · What can be quieted

The fix exists. It is being ignored.

The International Maritime Organization issued voluntary guidelines for underwater noise reduction in 2014. Revised in 2024. Adopted, in any meaningful form, by almost no one.

Quieter propellers exist. Slower transit speeds work — a 10% reduction in vessel speed cuts noise output by half. After 9/11, when North Atlantic shipping briefly collapsed, ambient noise in the Bay of Fundy dropped 6 decibels. Right whale stress hormones fell with it, measurably, within days.

The ocean can recover. It does not need decades. It needs a regulation with teeth.

"This is not a story about whales. It is a story about whether we are willing to be quieter."
